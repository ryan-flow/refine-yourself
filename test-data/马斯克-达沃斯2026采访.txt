Larry Fink: Elon, welcome to Davos. This is your first time here. What took you so long?

Elon Musk: Well, I've been a bit busy. But I think it's important to have these conversations about where we're heading. My goal across all my companies is really to maximize the future of civilization and help expand consciousness beyond Earth.

Larry Fink: Let's start with AI. You've been warning about it for years, and now you're building one of the biggest AI infrastructure projects in the world. Where are we really?

Elon Musk: We might have AI that is smarter than any human by the end of this year. And no later than next year. By 2030 or 2031, AI will be smarter than all of humanity collectively.

Larry Fink: That's an extraordinary claim. Walk me through the timeline.

Elon Musk: We've been in the recursive self-improvement stage for some time. Fully automated AI self-improvement may appear by the end of this year, at the latest next year. The rate of improvement is not linear — it's exponential. And honestly, most people don't grasp how fast this is moving.

Larry Fink: What does that mean for jobs and the economy?

Elon Musk: There will be such an abundance of goods and services because my prediction is there'll be more robots than people. The output of goods and services will far exceed money supply, causing deflation. Not inflation — deflation. In three years, advancements in AI and robotics will lead to a dramatic increase in output faster than money supply.

Larry Fink: So what happens to human purpose? If machines do everything, what do we do?

Elon Musk: Nothing's perfect, but you can't have both — work that has to be done and amazing abundance for all. I think in the future, working will be optional. People can play this back in 20 years and say it was wrong. But I believe that within 10 to 20 years, work becomes a choice, not a necessity.

Larry Fink: What about your Optimus robot? When can I buy one?

Elon Musk: Optimus 3 is completing its final stage right now. It's the most advanced robot in the world — no other product is even close. We'll start production this summer, initially low volume, and then high volume next year. We're planning annual production capacity of 1 million for Optimus 3, and 10 million for Optimus 4. We'll be selling them to the public by the end of this year or early next year.

Larry Fink: And the self-driving? Tesla's been promising that for a while.

Elon Musk: Full self-driving is essentially a solved problem at this point. We're rolling out robotaxis in several US cities right now. We hope to get European approval next month. The technology works. It's really about regulatory approval at this stage.

Larry Fink: What's the biggest bottleneck for AI right now? Is it chips, algorithms, or something else?

Elon Musk: The limiting factor for AI deployment is fundamentally electrical power. It's not chips. We're building a gigawatt-scale AI factory in Memphis right now. People don't realize that AI consumes enormous amounts of electricity. If you look at the growth curve, within a few years, AI could consume as much power as entire countries.

Larry Fink: So how do we solve the energy problem?

Elon Musk: Solar is the answer. A 100-mile by 100-mile square of solar panels could power the entire United States. That's not huge — that's a small corner of Nevada or Arizona. Tesla and SpaceX are deploying solar at about 100 gigawatts per year. And China is building solar faster than anyone. The problem is not technology — it's permitting and tariff barriers.

Larry Fink: You mentioned SpaceX. Tell me about Starship and what it means.

Elon Musk: Full reusability of Starship is the major breakthrough we're working on this year. Once we achieve that, the cost of access to space drops by 100 times. That changes everything. With Starship fully reusable, we can put enormous amounts of mass into orbit at a fraction of the current cost. We're planning 10,000 to 30,000 launches per year eventually.

Larry Fink: I've heard you talk about putting AI in space. What does that mean?

Elon Musk: Within a few years, we'll have solar-powered AI satellites. The idea is simple: in space, you have 24/7 solar power with no atmospheric interference. No clouds, no night. Within 30 to 36 months, space becomes the cheapest location for AI deployment. In 5 years, computing power in space will exceed the total on Earth.

Larry Fink: Let's talk about Mars. You've said you want to die there. Still true?

Elon Musk: Do I want to die on Mars? Yes, but just not on impact. But I want to be clear — early Mars settlement will be extremely dangerous. Much more dangerous than Earth. Mars is not an escape pod. If something goes wrong on Earth, Mars is not going to save you. But becoming a multi-planet species is essential for the long-term survival of consciousness.

Larry Fink: What about aging and longevity? Can we solve that?

Elon Musk: I think aging is a very solvable problem. There seems to be a synchronizing clock across all 35 trillion cells in the human body. If we can figure out that clock, we can potentially reverse aging. But I'm not sure extreme longevity is entirely positive. If people live for hundreds of years, society might ossify. New ideas come from new generations.

Larry Fink: You mentioned deflation earlier. Are you saying money will become worthless?

Elon Musk: Money will become unimportant at some point in the future. Future AI will not use human currency — it will only care about energy and mass: wattage and tonnage. That's the real economy. We're headed for incredible abundance, and the economic scale will grow tenfold in 10 years — assuming we avoid a major war.

Larry Fink: What advice would you give to people who are worried about all this change?

Elon Musk: I would encourage everyone to be optimistic and excited about the future. Technology has the potential to create unprecedented abundance. Better to err on the side of being an optimist and wrong rather than a pessimist and right. We're at the threshold of the most exciting period in human history.

Larry Fink: One last question — are we alone in the universe?

Elon Musk: Well, I can tell you that SpaceX has 9,000 satellites in orbit, and none of them have ever had to dodge alien spacecraft. So either they're not there, or they're very good at hiding. Personally, I think the Fermi paradox is one of the most important questions we need to answer. That's another reason we need to get to Mars.
